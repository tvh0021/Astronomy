ABOUT ME

I am a second-year Physics PhD. student at the University of North Texas. My current research focus primarily on examining the kinematics of stars and gas in the interstellar medium, particularly in molecular clouds, where the cold dense gas collapses to form stars. My PhD. advisor is Dr. Yuan Li.

Additionally, I am collaborating with M.S. students in the Artificial Intelligence program at UNT to utilize pre-trained deep neural networks to identify similar regions within the Milky Way. At other times, you can also find me studying the physical properties of weak emission-line quasars. Further details about each of my project can be found in the Research section.

Outside of research, I enjoy travelling, building computers, and solving Rubik's cubes. I also do landscape photography, and on the rare occasions with ideal condition, astrophotography. Consequently, images on this website were taken by me.

I grew up in Ho Chi Minh city, Vietnam, before moving to the US as a cultural exchange student in 2014. After high school, I obtained an A.S. from Central Arizona College in Coolidge, Arizona in May, 2017. I graduated with a B.S. in Physics at the University of Rochester in Rochester, New York in May, 2020.

Tracing Turbulence in the Interstellar Medium with Young Stars


Stars are born out of the turbulent molecular gas of the interstellar medium. Once formed, they decouple from the surrounding gas, but still retain their pre-natal kinematics. Using Gaia astrometric measurements in conjunction with APOGEE-2, we calculate the 3-dimensional velocity structure function of stars in four nearby star-forming regions and compare them to the stucture functions found in Hα and CO gas.
In our recently-published paper in ApJ Letters, we found that the stucture functions of groups in the Orion Molecular Cloud Complex exhibit features of turbulence consistent with Larson's Law of velocity dispersion. We also found evidence of local energy injection at the 70 pc scale, which supports claims that a supernova took place at the heart of Orion ~6 Myr ago.
Read our press release here. Watch our interview for the American Astronomical Society's Journal Author Series here.
This project was carried out under supervision from Dr. Yuan Li of UNT.

Similarity Mapping of the Milky Way Using Neural Style Transfer

As a graduate student advisor, I help M.S. students in the A.I. summer program at UNT apply the pre-trained VGG-19 deep neural network architecture to the Milky Way map from the Wisconsin H-Alpha Mapper survey. Using the Orion Molecular Cloud Complex as a reference image, neural style transfer was able to identify various star-forming regions in the Milky Way. This result shows neural style transfer's promise as a quick targets identification pipeline for future sky surveys.
Read our project summary poster here.
This project was carried out under supervision from Dr. Mark Albert and Dr. Yuan Li of UNT.

Weak Emission-Line Quasars (WLQs) in the Context of C Ⅳ Emission Line Properties


WLQs are a peculiar subset of luminous active galactic nuclei whose emission spectra show extremely weak emission line profiles of Lyα+N Ⅴ λ1240 and/or C Ⅳ λ1549. Much effort has been invested to explain the weakness in emission profiles of these quasars over the last two decades. My current project proposes a new way of looking at WLQs as simply objects with high accretion rates (roughly equal to or larger than the Eddington limit), and that their intrinsic line profile weakness is only one factor in a two-parameter correlation called the "C Ⅳ distance".
This project was carried out under supervision from Dr. Ohad Shemmer of UNT.

Generating Initial Data for Binary Neutron Stars Merger Simulations


I worked as an undergraduate research student at the Rochester Institute of Technology's Center for Computational Relativity and Gravitation from 2018 to 2020. There, I modified and documented the LORENE initial data code to generate physically motivated binary neutron stars pre-merger. Furthermore, I performed several GRMHD simulations of binary neutron stars merger using the Einstein Toolkit, and reported our findings at the 2019 Midwest Relativity Meeting in Grand Rapids, Michigan.
